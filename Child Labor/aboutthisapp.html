About This App

What is this app?

Sweat & Toil: Child Labor, Forced Labor, and Human Trafficking Around the World is a comprehensive resource developed by the U.S. Department of Labor (USDOL) documenting child labor and forced labor worldwide. Data and research in this app are taken from ILAB’s three flagship reports: Findings on the Worst Forms of Child Labor; List of Goods Produced by Child Labor or Forced Labor; and List of Products Produced by Forced or Indentured Child Labor. This app fits these three information-packed reports the size of a phone book in the palm of your hand. Key things you can do with this app are:

  1. Check countries’ efforts to eliminate child labor;
  2. Find child labor data;
  3. Browse goods produced with child labor or forced labor;
  4. Review laws, ratifications, and enforcement efforts; and
  5. See what governments, businesses, and consumers can to do end child labor.

What is the purpose of these reports?

As a starting point for action, these reports are intended to raise public awareness about child labor and forced labor around the world and to promote and inform efforts to address them. The research helps inform Congress and Executive Branch agencies that formulate labor and trade policy and is an important resource for USDOL in assessing future technical assistance and research priorities as it seeks to combat child labor around the world. It is also a valuable resource for foreign governments, researchers, advocacy organizations, and companies wishing to carry out risk assessments and engage in due diligence on labor rights in their supply chains.

How can I use this app?

Using this app can be a starting point to empower yourself with knowledge about child labor or forced labor around the world. Anyone with an interest in these issues – governments, businesses, academics, civil society, consumers – can use it as a source of information to begin asking questions, taking action, and demanding change. And because this app makes this information both portable and available offline, you can use it in any corner of the world, for on-the-ground engagement or education on these issues. We are also making the data available to developers to use in their own applications, data visualizations, and mashups with other data sets through our API.

Are there new features to the app?

Sweat & Toil 2.0 is now in its second year, and contains the latest information from the 2015 reporting period. New features include navigation filters on Country and Goods list pages, comprehensive legal standards data, and an all new enforcement section which includes detailed data on labor and criminal enforcement efforts.

What’s in the Legal Standards Section?

The Legal Standards Section contains information on national laws which exist to protect children from child labor and its worst forms. This includes minimum age laws and those relating to working conditions, the worst forms of child labor, military service, and education.

  • Yes/No reflects whether a country’s laws establish a specific legal standard;
  • An asterisk connotes gaps in the legal framework when laws are established;
  • The data appears green when a country’s laws establish basic protections for children against child labor. When the data appears red, a country’s legislative framework lacks basic protections, either due to the absence of laws or gaps in existing laws.

For more information, consult the country profile linked to the country page, under Report PDF.

What’s in the Enforcement Section?

For the first time, you can access detailed labor and criminal law enforcement data from 2014 and 2015. Labor data includes information about the labor inspectorate’s financial and human resources, authority to conduct inspections and assess penalties, and actions and mechanisms to enforce labor laws, including those related to child labor. Criminal data includes information about actions and mechanisms to enforce laws related to the worst forms of child labor.

How do I determine the exploitation types that are known to exist in the production of certain goods?

The Exploitation Type page gives an overview of all goods listed in the TVPRA and EO reports. Icons represent child labor, forced (adult) labor, and forced child labor. Filter buttons provide a quick way to examine specific exploitation types and their associated goods. Forced child labor (FCL) is a type of child labor (CL), so the filter for forced child labor will also pick up child labor. However, when all three exploitation types exist, only FL and FCL will appear.

Why isn’t every country in the world represented here?

This has to do with the data availability and the scope of Congressional mandates that govern USDOL’s reporting on these issues. For more information about these mandates and the three reports this app draws from, read our methodology page.
